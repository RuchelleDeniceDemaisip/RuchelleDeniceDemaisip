Heatwave Grips Iloilo City as Temperatures Soar

In recent years, residents of Iloilo City have been feeling the heat like never before, with hot days becoming increasingly hotter and more frequent, while cooler temperatures become a rarity. The combination of high temperatures and humidity, typical of the Philippine climate, poses significant risks to public health and safety.

Last week, Mayor Jerry Treñas took decisive action in response to the extreme heat, issuing Executive Order No. 047-B to extend the suspension of in-person classes to colleges and universities across the city. This decision came as the heat index soared above 40 degrees Celsius, making outdoor activities unbearable for many residents.

On April 4th, Iloilo City recorded its highest heat index of the year at a scorching 42 degrees Celsius, according to data from the Manila Observatory/Shell Philippines Automatic Weather Station. Donna Magno, head of the City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office, emphasized the urgency of addressing the heatwave and its potential consequences.

In an interview with Shenna Borja, a student and call center agent, she expressed the challenges of falling asleep during the daytime. Despite the relief of canceled classes, Shenna struggles to rest properly due to the absence of air conditioning in her home. The intense heat not only causes discomfort but also leads to headaches, making it difficult for her to focus during evening work shifts due to inadequate sleep.

“At times, I feel like crying because my job is crucial to me, but the unbearable heat has been severely impacting my performance. I've been struggling to maintain productivity, and the thought of enduring another day without relief is overwhelming. I'm considering installing an air conditioner in my room, as I can't continue like this. It's frustrating feeling so helpless,” she added.

Despite the sweltering conditions, many residents are left with no choice but to brave the heat to carry out essential errands. Others are taking advantage of the cancellation of face-to-face classes by seeking relief at beaches and other cooling spots.

Note: The effects of extreme heat extend far beyond discomfort. Heatwaves can increase the risk of other types of disasters, exacerbate drought conditions, and create wildfire hazards. Even healthy adults are not immune to the dangers of extreme heat, with heat-related illnesses such as heat cramps, heat exhaustion, and heatstroke posing significant risks.

Agriculture also suffers, with high temperatures impacting plant growth and livestock experiencing heat stress. The province of Iloilo bore the brunt of the impact, experiencing losses totaling P519 million in agriculture trailed by Antique with P130 million, Negros Occidental with P78 million, Aklan with P30 million, and Capiz with P12 million.

The root of the problem lies in human-induced climate change, driven by greenhouse gas emissions from human activities. While heatwaves are a natural phenomenon, they are worsening due to global warming, according to experts from NASA, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, and the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change.

In response to the heatwave, authorities urge residents to take precautions to stay safe. Keeping cool and staying hydrated are essential during periods of extreme heat, along with avoiding outdoor activities during the hottest parts of the day.
